### [Andrej Karpathy talks about "Claws"](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Feb/21/claws/#atom-everything) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Andrej Karpathy, a leading AI researcher, publicly discussed 'Claws' as an emerging architectural layer in AI systems, specifically highlighting that just as LLM agents were a layer on top of LLMs, Claws are a new orchestration layer on top of LLM agents. He pointed to specific implementations like NanoClaw, which features a minimal core engine of roughly 4000 lines of code and runs agents in containers by default. This matters because it signals the formalization of a new, critical component in the AI stack focused on advanced orchestration, which could standardize how complex, persistent AI agents are built and managed. Karpathy's endorsement lends significant credibility to the concept, potentially accelerating adoption and development within the AI community and influencing the design of future agentic systems. A key technical detail is the emphasis on minimal, auditable codebases, with NanoClaw's core being small enough to fit 'into both my head and that of AI agents,' promoting manageability and security. Furthermore, the ecosystem is already diversifying with various implementations like nanobot, zeroclaw, and picoclaw, indicating rapid experimentation around this architectural concept.

*Source: rss/Simon Willison*

**Background**: LLM agents are AI systems that use large language models (LLMs) to reason, plan, and execute actions using tools or APIs, moving beyond simple text generation to performing tasks. Orchestration refers to the software layer that manages these agents, handling complex workflows, scheduling, context management, and persistence across interactions. The discussion assumes familiarity with the ongoing evolution of 'agentic' AI systems, where autonomy and multi-step task completion are key goals.

### [Taalas serves Llama 3.1 8B at 17,000 tokens/second](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Feb/20/taalas/#atom-everything) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Canadian hardware startup Taalas announced its first product, a custom hardware implementation of Meta's Llama 3.1 8B model that can run inference at 17,000 tokens per second. This performance is achieved using their 'Silicon Llama' design, which employs aggressive quantization techniques combining 3-bit and 6-bit parameters. This represents a major breakthrough in efficient AI deployment, as such extreme inference speeds could enable real-time, high-volume applications of large language models that were previously impractical. It demonstrates the potential for custom silicon to dramatically outperform general-purpose hardware like GPUs for specific AI workloads, potentially lowering the cost and energy footprint of running LLMs at scale. The company's current implementation uses a mixed-precision approach with 3-bit and 6-bit parameters, and they have indicated their next generation will move to 4-bit quantization. A live demo of the technology is available for users to test at chatjimmy.ai.

*Source: rss/Simon Willison*

**Background**: Quantization is a technique that reduces the precision of a neural network's parameters, typically stored as 32-bit or 16-bit floating-point values, into lower-bit formats like 8-bit, 4-bit, or even lower. This process significantly reduces the memory footprint and computational requirements of a model, enabling faster inference and deployment on less powerful or specialized hardware, though it often involves a trade-off with model accuracy. Hardware acceleration for LLM inference is an active field of research and development, with approaches using FPGAs, ASICs, and custom silicon aiming to outperform general-purpose GPUs in terms of speed and efficiency for specific model architectures.

### [Quoting Thariq Shihipar](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Feb/20/thariq-shihipar/#atom-everything) ⭐️ 7.0/10

An Anthropic engineer, Thariq Shihipar, revealed that prompt caching is a foundational technique enabling the economic viability of long-running AI agent products like Claude Code by reusing computation from previous interactions to significantly reduce latency and cost. He further explained that the system's prompt cache hit rate is so critical to business operations that low rates trigger SEV (Site Event) alerts and directly influence the generosity of rate limits for subscription plans. This insight matters because it reveals how core infrastructure optimizations like caching are not just performance tweaks but fundamental business enablers for expensive, stateful AI applications, directly linking technical efficiency to product pricing and customer experience. It highlights the operational reality that running large-scale AI agents requires sophisticated engineering to manage costs, which in turn dictates commercial decisions like rate limits and subscription tiers. A key technical detail is that the entire Claude Code "harness" is built around this prompt caching strategy, indicating it's a first-class architectural principle, not an afterthought. Furthermore, the system employs proactive monitoring where the cache hit rate is a key performance indicator (KPI) severe enough to warrant SEV declarations, showing its direct impact on system health and business metrics.

*Source: rss/Simon Willison*

**Background**: Prompt caching is an optimization technique for Large Language Models (LLMs) where the computed results (often as key-value tensors) for identical or similar prompt prefixes are stored and reused, avoiding redundant and expensive computation for every new request. In production AI systems, SEV (Site Event) alerts are high-priority notifications triggered by critical issues that impact service stability or key business metrics. Rate limits are controls placed on API usage to manage costs, prevent abuse, and ensure fair resource allocation among users, often tiered based on subscription plans or usage levels.

This matters because a significant number of open-source projects rely on Discord for support and communication, making them vulnerable to policy changes on a proprietary platform, and it highlights a broader trend of regulatory pressure on social media platforms to implement age verification. The Matrix protocol, a leading federated alternative, has seen a spike in sign-ups to its matrix.org homeserver, but users often need to try multiple client applications as many are missing specific features despite all supporting basic chat and joining rooms.

*Source: rss/daroc*

**Background**: Discord is a popular, centralized, proprietary chat platform widely used by online communities. Federated communication protocols, like Matrix, operate on a network of independent servers (homeservers) that can interoperate, giving users more control and avoiding reliance on a single company. Age verification for online platforms is an industry-wide challenge, with companies like Meta and Google also developing AI models to estimate user ages, often in response to regulatory pressures from governments like the UK.
